What to Include on an Invoice — The 9 Essential Fields

7 min read · Updated 2025 · InvoiceForge Team
In This Article

  2. Optional Fields Worth Adding
  3. Common Invoice Mistakes to Avoid
  4. Legal Requirements by Country

A professional invoice isn't just a payment request — it's a legal document. Missing a key field can delay payment, cause confusion with a client's accounts payable team, or create tax headaches down the line. Here's everything you need to include, and why each field matters.

The 9 Essential Invoice Fields

1
Your Business Information

Your full legal name or business name, address, email, and phone number. This tells the client who to pay and how to contact you if they have questions about the invoice.

Acme Design LLC · 123 Main St, Austin TX 78701 · hello@acmedesign.com
2
Client Information (Bill To)

The full name of the person or company you're billing, their address, and contact details. For larger companies, include the department or contact name to ensure it reaches the right person.

Attn: Sarah Johnson · Accounts Payable · TechCorp Inc
3
Invoice Number

A unique identifier for this invoice. Use a consistent format and increment sequentially. This is critical for tracking, dispute resolution, and tax filing. Never reuse invoice numbers.

INV-0047 · or · 2025-047 · or · TC-2025-001 (client initials + year + sequence)
4
Invoice Date

The date the invoice was issued. This is the start date for payment terms — "Net 30" means payment is due 30 days from this date. Use a consistent date format (MM/DD/YYYY or spelled out).

January 15, 2025
5
Due Date

The date by which payment must be received. Always specify this explicitly — "Net 30" in the terms section is easily overlooked, but a clear "Payment due: February 14, 2025" is hard to miss.

February 14, 2025 (Net 30)
6
Itemized List of Services or Products

Each line item should include a description, quantity, rate, and line total. Be specific — vague descriptions like "design work" invite disputes. Specificity builds trust and speeds up payment approval.

Homepage redesign — wireframes + 3 concepts + 2 revisions · 12 hrs @ $125/hr = $1,500
7
Subtotal, Tax, and Total

Show your subtotal before tax, then tax as a separate line item with the rate applied (e.g., "Sales Tax 8.25%"), then the grand total. Never bury tax in the line items — it creates accounting problems for your client.

Subtotal $1,500 · Tax (8.25%) $123.75 · Total Due $1,623.75
8
Payment Terms

How and when you expect to be paid. Include accepted payment methods (bank transfer, PayPal, check, etc.), your bank details if applicable, and your late fee policy. This section protects you legally.

Payment via ACH or check. Invoices past due 30 days subject to 1.5%/month late fee.
9
Notes

Optional but recommended. Use this for a thank-you message, project reference, or any relevant context. A brief "Thank you for your business — it's a pleasure working with you" goes a long way.

Reference: Q1 2025 Website Project · Thank you for your business!

Optional Fields Worth Adding

Depending on your industry or client, these additional fields can prevent confusion and speed up payment:

  • PO Number — many corporate clients require a purchase order number before their AP team will process payment
  • Project/Reference Name — helps both parties match the invoice to a specific engagement
  • Discount — for returning clients or early-payment incentives
  • Amount Paid / Balance Due — useful for partial-payment situations or deposit invoices
  • Your logo — adds professionalism and brand recognition

Common Invoice Mistakes to Avoid

  • No due date — "Net 30" buried in the terms is easy to miss. Always put an explicit date.
  • Vague descriptions — "Consulting services — $2,000" will get questioned. Be specific.
  • Wrong client name — invoicing the wrong entity can create legal and tax problems. Confirm the exact billing name before sending.
  • Forgetting your own contact info — clients need to reach you if something is unclear.
  • No invoice number — makes tracking and re-sending painful.
  • Inconsistent date formats — pick one (MM/DD/YYYY or spelled out) and stick to it.

Legal Requirements by Country

Most countries have minimum legal requirements for business invoices, particularly when VAT or GST is involved:

  • United States — No federal requirements for invoice format, but state sales tax rules vary. Include your sales tax registration number if charging sales tax.
  • European Union — VAT invoices must include your VAT registration number, the client's VAT number (for B2B), the VAT rate, and VAT amount as a separate line.
  • United Kingdom — Post-Brexit, similar to EU rules for VAT-registered businesses. Must include your VAT number if VAT-registered.
  • Canada — GST/HST invoices over $30 must include your GST/HST registration number and the applicable tax rate.
  • Australia — Tax invoices must include your ABN and clearly show the GST amount.

Note: InvoiceForge is not a tax advisor. If you have questions about your specific tax obligations, consult a local accountant or tax professional.
